## What Is Kakutei Shinkoku?

**Kakutei shinkoku (確定申告)** literally means "final tax declaration." It's Japan's annual income tax return process, where individuals report their income and calculate the tax they owe — or the refund they're entitled to — for the previous year.

- **Who runs it:** Japan's National Tax Agency (NTA / 国税庁)
- **Filing period:** February 16 – March 15 each year (for the prior calendar year's income)
- **Tax year:** January 1 – December 31

Japan operates on a pay-as-you-go system for most salaried workers, where employers withhold income tax monthly and reconcile everything at year-end through a process called **nenmatsu chosei (年末調整)**. If you're a standard company employee with no other income, your employer handles your taxes for you — and you may not need to file kakutei shinkoku at all.

But if you're a freelancer, have side income, changed jobs mid-year, or want to claim certain deductions, you'll need to file yourself.

## Do Foreigners in Japan Need to File?

Residency status — not nationality — determines your tax obligations in Japan.

**You likely need to file if:**

- ✅ You're a **freelancer or self-employed** (個人事業主) with any income
- ✅ You have **side income exceeding ¥200,000** (副業 20万円超), even if your employer does nenmatsu chosei
- ✅ You have **income from overseas** (investments, remote work for a foreign company, rental income abroad)
- ✅ You **changed jobs** during the year and your year-end adjustment wasn't completed
- ✅ You **left Japan mid-year** and need to settle your tax liability
- ✅ You want to claim deductions not handled by your employer — like large medical expenses, furusato nozei (hometown tax donations), or housing loan deductions
- ✅ You earned income from **multiple employers**

**You probably don't need to file if:**

- ❌ You have a single employer who did nenmatsu chosei for you
- ❌ You have no other income outside that employment
- ❌ You don't want to claim any additional deductions

When in doubt, file. There's no penalty for filing unnecessarily, but there are penalties for *not* filing when you should have.

## Blue Form vs. White Form: 青色申告 vs. 白色申告

This is one of the most confusing distinctions for foreign freelancers in Japan — and also one of the most financially consequential.

### White Form (白色申告 — Shiro-iro Shinkoku)

The white form is the **default, simpler option**. Anyone can use it. Record-keeping requirements are minimal — you just need to track income and basic expenses.

- No pre-approval needed
- Simpler bookkeeping
- **No special deductions available**

### Blue Form (青色申告 — Ao-iro Shinkoku)

The blue form requires **pre-registration with your local tax office** (you apply in advance using a form called 青色申告承認申請書). You also need to maintain proper double-entry bookkeeping records throughout the year.

In exchange, you get access to significant tax advantages:

- **¥650,000 special deduction** (青色申告特別控除) if you file electronically via e-Tax with proper records — this alone can save you ¥100,000+ in taxes
- **¥550,000 deduction** if you file by paper
- ¥100,000 deduction if you use simplified single-entry bookkeeping
- Ability to carry forward business losses for up to **3 years**
- Ability to deduct a **family member's salary** as a business expense (if they work for your business)

**Our recommendation:** If you're a freelancer or self-employed in Japan for the long term, blue form (青色申告) is almost always worth the extra setup. The ¥650,000 deduction pays for itself within the first year in most cases. You'll need accounting software (like freee or MF Cloud) to make the bookkeeping manageable.

*Side note: If you're just starting out and missed the blue form registration deadline (it must be filed by March 15 or within 2 months of starting your business), start with white form this year and register for blue form for next year.*

## Step-by-Step: How to Actually File

There are three ways to file kakutei shinkoku in Japan.

### Option 1 — e-Tax (Online Filing)

e-Tax is Japan's online tax filing system. It's the recommended method for most people once you've set it up — you can file from home, receive your refund faster, and if you're on blue form, qualify for the full ¥650,000 deduction.

**What you need:**
- My Number Card (マイナンバーカード) — the physical IC card, not just the notification letter
- Either: a smartphone with the "My Number Card" app (most modern iPhones/Androids work), OR a USB IC card reader for your computer
- An e-Tax account (register at the NTA website)

**Steps:**
1. Register for e-Tax at [e-tax.nta.go.jp](https://www.e-tax.nta.go.jp/)
2. Set up authentication via your My Number Card
3. Use the "Kakutei Shinkoku Sakusei Corner" (確定申告書等作成コーナー) — a step-by-step form wizard
4. Enter your income, deductions, and bank account for refund
5. Submit electronically and save your confirmation number

**The honest truth about e-Tax:** The interface is mostly Japanese, and the card reader setup can be genuinely painful — especially on Mac or with non-standard browsers. Give yourself at least 2–3 hours for your first time. Safari on Mac tends to work better than Chrome.

### Option 2 — Paper Filing at a Tax Office (確定申告会場)

During filing season (February–March), NTA opens dedicated filing centers (確定申告会場) in most cities. You can walk in, fill out paper forms, and submit on the spot.

**Bring with you:**
- My Number card (or notification letter + photo ID)
- Source documents: payment slips (源泉徴収票 from employers), bank statements showing interest income, receipts for deductions you're claiming
- Your bank account information (for receiving refunds)
- A pen

**Tips:**
- Morning weekdays are less crowded; avoid the week before March 15
- Some offices have multilingual staff or interpretation services — call ahead to ask
- Bring a Japanese-speaking friend or use a translation app if needed
- Tax office staff can help you fill out the forms, but they won't give tax advice

### Option 3 — Tax Accountant (税理士)

If your financial situation is complex — you own a business, have investments in multiple countries, have rental income, or are managing an inheritance — hiring a tax accountant (税理士, *zeirishi*) is worth considering.

**Typical fees:**
- Basic kakutei shinkoku: ¥30,000–¥60,000
- Complex situations (multiple income sources, corporate): ¥80,000–¥150,000+
- Annual contract (quarterly bookkeeping + annual filing): ¥120,000–¥300,000/year

Finding an English-speaking tax accountant in Japan isn't easy, but they exist — especially in Tokyo and Osaka. The Japan Federation of Certified Public Tax Accountants ([nichizeiren.or.jp](https://www.nichizeiren.or.jp/)) has a directory.

## Key Deductions Foreigners Often Miss

This is where real money gets left on the table. Japan's tax code offers numerous deductions, but the official documentation is almost entirely in Japanese, so many foreigners unknowingly overpay.

### Medical Expense Deduction (医療費控除)
If you (or your household members) paid more than ¥100,000 in medical expenses in the year, you can deduct the excess. This includes doctor visits, prescription drugs, dental work, and certain OTC medications. Save every receipt — you'll need to tally them up.

### Life Insurance Premium Deduction (生命保険料控除)
Premiums paid to Japanese life insurance, annuity, or long-term care insurance policies can be deducted, up to certain limits (¥40,000–¥120,000 depending on the type of policy and when it started).

### Social Insurance Premium Deduction (社会保険料控除)
If you're enrolled in National Health Insurance (国民健康保険) or National Pension (国民年金) and paying premiums directly (not through an employer), these are 100% deductible. Many freelancers don't realize this.

### Furusato Nozei (ふるさと納税)
Japan's hometown tax donation program lets you donate to municipalities and receive return gifts while effectively reducing your tax burden. If you used the One-Stop Special Procedure (ワンストップ特例), you don't need to file kakutei shinkoku for this — but if you're already filing anyway, including your furusato donations can be advantageous.

### Housing Loan Deduction (住宅ローン控除)
If you took out a mortgage on a home in Japan, you may qualify for a significant annual tax credit (住宅ローン控除). This must be claimed through kakutei shinkoku for the first year; after that, salaried employees can usually handle it through nenmatsu chosei.

### Small Business Deduction for Blue Form Filers (青色申告特別控除)
As covered above — if you're on blue form and file via e-Tax, you deduct ¥650,000 from your business income. This is not a small amount.

## Common Mistakes Foreigners Make

### 1. Assuming You Don't Need to File
"My company does nenmatsu chosei, so I'm covered." True — for your *salary* income. But if you have any side income, freelance payments, or investment gains, those are separate. The ¥200,000 threshold is per year; if your side income was ¥250,000, you needed to file.

### 2. Not Reporting Overseas Income
Japan taxes **worldwide income** for tax residents (permanent residents and those who have lived in Japan for more than 5 of the past 10 years). If you're receiving income from a foreign company, investment account, or rental property overseas, it needs to be declared. Japan has tax treaties with many countries to prevent double-taxation, but you still need to report.

### 3. Waiting Until March 14 to Set Up e-Tax
The My Number Card authentication, browser compatibility issues, and registration process can take 2–3 hours on a good day. People who wait until the last week of filing season often can't submit in time and have to go in person.

### 4. Not Keeping Receipts Throughout the Year
For blue form filers especially: you need documentation for every business expense you claim. This means keeping receipts for equipment, software subscriptions, home office costs, transportation for business purposes, and professional development. Reconstruct them from bank/credit card statements if needed, but it's painful.

### 5. Mixing Up the 20万円 and 38万円 Rules
Two separate thresholds confuse people constantly:
- ¥200,000: If your side income *exceeds* this, you must file kakutei shinkoku (even if your employer handles your salary taxes)
- ¥380,000: The basic personal deduction (基礎控除) available to everyone — reduces your taxable income automatically

### 6. Forgetting to File if You Left Japan Mid-Year
If you emigrated during the year, you were still a tax resident while in Japan and may owe taxes on income earned during that period. You either file before you leave (appointing a tax representative) or file a final return as a non-resident. This catches people off guard.

## FAQ

### Q1: I'm a foreigner on a work visa. Do I have to pay Japanese income tax?
Yes. If you live in Japan (regardless of visa type or nationality), you're subject to Japanese income tax on income earned in Japan. If you've lived in Japan for more than 5 of the past 10 years, you're also taxed on worldwide income.

### Q2: My company does nenmatsu chosei — do I still need to file?
Only if you have additional income beyond your salary (freelance, investments, side jobs), if you changed employers during the year, if you want to claim deductions your employer can't process (large medical expenses, housing loans), or if you had multiple employers. For most single-employer salaried workers with no side income, nenmatsu chosei is sufficient.

### Q3: I missed the March 15 deadline. What now?
File as soon as possible. Late filing (期限後申告) is still accepted and is far better than not filing at all. There are late penalties — a surcharge of 5% if you voluntarily file late, and 15% if the NTA discovers you haven't filed — but filing late and paying the penalty is better than ignoring it. There's no penalty for late filing if you're owed a refund.

### Q4: Can I file in English?
Not officially — all NTA forms are in Japanese. However, the NTA English portal provides guides and instructions in English, and e-Tax's form wizard has some English guidance. For hands-on help navigating Japanese-language fields in plain English, tools like Japan Expat AI Assistant can walk you through each section.

### Q5: What's the difference between 源泉徴収 (withholding tax) and kakutei shinkoku?
Withholding tax (源泉徴収, *gensen chōshū*) is tax deducted at the source — your employer takes it out of your paycheck monthly. Kakutei shinkoku is the annual true-up: you reconcile your actual tax liability against what was withheld. If too much was withheld, you get a refund. If too little, you pay the difference.

### Q6: Do I need to report my overseas bank accounts or assets?
If you held ¥50 million or more in overseas financial assets at any point during the year, you must file a Report on Foreign Financial Assets (国外財産調書). Below that threshold, you don't need to file a special form — but any *income* from those assets (interest, dividends, capital gains) still needs to be declared on your kakutei shinkoku.

### Q7: I'm on a Working Holiday visa. Do tax rules apply to me differently?
Working Holiday visa holders are taxed the same as any other tax resident. If you're working and earning income in Japan, you owe income tax. If you've been in Japan for the full calendar year, you file kakutei shinkoku by March 15 for the prior year. If you arrived or left partway through the year, you file for the portion of the year you were a resident.
